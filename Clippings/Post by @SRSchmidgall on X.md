Doctors have known for decades: the clinical interview is the most important diagnostic tool

**Post 1**

Doctors have known for decades: the clinical interview is the most important diagnostic tool. Turns out, the same is true for AI. In work led by @breda_joe, @jesunshine, and @danmcduff, we randomized 13,917 Fitbit users across 5 AI strategies for symptom assessment.

---

**Post 2**

The key finding: HOW the AI talks to patients matters more than most people think. AI that interviews patients (structured follow-ups, guided questions) significantly outperformed AI that lets users describe symptoms freely (p < 0.001).

---

**Post 3**

In blinded evaluation, clinicians ranked SymptomAI diagnoses #1 in 52.9% of cases vs 23.5% for independent clinicians (p < 0.001). SymptomAI DDx also had significantly higher odds of matching the true diagnosis (OR = 2.47).

---

**Post 4**

This wasn't just a benchmark:

- 13,917 real Fitbit users, real symptoms
- 1,228 clinician-confirmed diagnoses
- 517 cases with full expert panel review (250+ hrs)
- Validated on separate US panel (N=1,509)
- 9-month live deployment via Fitbit app

---

**Post 5**

We also used SymptomAI labels to analyze 500,000+ days of wearable data across ~400 conditions. Influenza alone showed OR > 7 for wearable-detectable physiological shifts. Conversational AI + continuous sensing is an underexplored frontier.

---

**Post 6**

Among 1,546 users with respiratory infections, wearable biosignals shifted days BEFORE they reported symptoms. Heart rate ↑, sleep disrupted, steps ↓ — and all visible in the data before patients even opened the app.

---

**Post 7**

The takeaway: conversation design is an underexplored lever for improving medical AI. The shift from "responding to patients" to "interviewing patients" is large and measurable. Every AI chatbot handling health queries should take note.

Full paper: [SymptomAI: Towards a Conversational AI Agent for Everyday Symptom...](https://arxiv.org/abs/SymptomAI)
